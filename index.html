Alan Mathison Turing

The father of theoretical Computer Science and Cryptography

Here is a time line of Alan Turing's life, whose work was key to breaking the wartime Enigma codes:

  • 1912 - Born on June 23rd, in Paddington, London, United Kingdom.
  • 1926 - Alan Turing starts his studies at St. Michael's Preparatory School, St. Leonards-on-Sea.
  • 1931 - Turing attends Sherborne School, where he excels in mathematics, science, and running.
  • 1936 - On November 12th, Turing publishes the seminal paper titled "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem," in which he introduces the concept of a universal machine capable of solving mathematical problems.
  • 1938 - Alan Turing enters King's College, University of Cambridge, where he continues his work in mathematics and logic.
  • 1939 - With the outbreak of World War II, Turing joins the British war effort at Bletchley Park, working in the team that decrypts the codes of the Enigma cryptographic machine used by the Nazis.
  • 1940 - Turing leads the team that develops the "Bombe," an electromechanical machine to aid in breaking the Enigma codes, becoming crucial for the Allies during the war.
  • 1945 - World War II comes to an end, and Turing's work and the team at Bletchley Park are instrumental in the Allies victory.
  • 1948 - Turing works on designing a general-purpose digital computer at the National Physical Laboratory in Manchester, known as the "Manchester Mark 1".
  • 1950 - Turing publishes a paper titled "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," where he proposes the "Turing Test" as a measure of a machine's intelligence.
  • 1952 - Alan Turing is convicted of "gross indecency" after admitting to a homosexual relationship. He undergoes hormone therapy as an alternative to imprisonment, which severely affects his physical and mental health.
  • 1954 - On June 7th, Alan Turing is found dead in his home. The cause of death is officially ruled as suicide by cyanide poisoning.
